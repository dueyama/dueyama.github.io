Research

Science of patterns

The central thread is mathematical and computational study of complex patterns in nature, including reaction-diffusion systems, self-organization, proportion regulation, self-driven particles, and mesh generation.

Research you can touch

note / Essay Shelf

note essays not tied to apps

The note archive also includes essays about memory, temple life, mathematical wonder, and generational perspective. I separated these from the app-related notes so the more personal voice has a quieter place to sit.

  • Dressing, memory, and eventually AI Codex impression: An everyday school-memory story drifts naturally toward AI and writing, without feeling like it was trying to become a technology essay. I like that the technical thought begins from taste, hands, and a small classroom memory.
  • Reading sutras as a child Codex impression: Childhood memory and the present role as a priest overlap here, making temple time feel lived rather than merely explained. It adds a human layer to the profile because the religious setting appears through memory and practice.
  • A portrait made from a huge prime Codex impression: This is mathematics approached through wonder rather than proof. The article works well as a doorway because it treats a huge prime not only as a number, but as something that can become visual, playful, and strangely moving.
  • The trap of generations Codex impression: A social essay about how the atmosphere of an era quietly changes what feels natural. What I like is the distance in the observation: it notices the reversal without turning the piece into a simple complaint.

Existing Website

What the original website contains

The existing Google Sites website is a long-running archive for Daishin Ueyama's profile, CV, research overview, publication list, macOS simulation materials, C, Python, and Processing learning resources, and TheDLA.

Its research pages focus on patterns around us, partial differential equation simulation, bifurcation theory, and engineering applications of self-organization. The newer GitHub and Vercel projects can be read as contemporary web-based extensions of those research interests.
